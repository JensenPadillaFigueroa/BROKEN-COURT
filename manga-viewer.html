Chapter 1 — Act 1: Roots
"The Broken Court"
We meet Yadi Rodríguez, a 16-year-old with supernatural tennis talent, trapped on a Caribbean island with no resources. A casual encounter with a stranger named Yen reveals for the first time that Yadi has something special.
Page 1 — Opening Splash
"In a world where giants rule the court..."
"...sometimes thunder is born from silence."
Isla Brava — Caribbean tropical island. Palm trees, colorful houses faded by sun and sea salt. In the center of the barrio: a cracked tennis court invaded by tropical weeds. On the court wall: a massive faded mural of Luna Borín — the local heroine who won Olympic gold.
The Mural — Luna Borín
Luna Borín's faded mural — eaten by years of sun and sea salt, but still recognizable: a young woman raising a gold medal. Proof you can make it off the island.
Page 2 — Don Emilio Watching
Don Emilio: "This kid again... He's been at it for 3 hours."
Don Emilio sits on an old bench at the edge of the court. A machete leans beside him — for cutting the weeds that keep invading the court. He watches Yadi with squinted eyes through cigarette smoke.
Pages 3-4 — Yadi Practicing Alone
Yadi stops, breathing hard, wipes sweat with his old faded shirt. Looks at the wall as if it were an opponent. His eyes — dark, intense — have a spark of something contained.
Don Emilio "Hey, kid! It's already 7! Don't you have a home?"
Yadi "Coming, Don Emilio. One more."
Don Emilio: "He always says 'one more'..."
Page 4 — The Last Shot
Yadi hits the "last" ball. This time the shot is different — cleaner, faster. The ball hits the wall with a sharp, loud sound.
CRACK.
Don Emilio raises his eyebrows. The cigarette nearly falls from his mouth. He looks at the mark the ball left on the wall — it's deep. He touches the dent with his fingers.
Don Emilio: "What the hell...?"
The Cracked Wall with Mural
The ball mark on the wall — deeper than any normal shot should leave. Next to Luna Borín's faded mural.
Pages 5-6 — Flashback: Young Yadi Watching TV
Young Yadi, 8 years old, sitting on the floor in front of an old small TV. On the screen: the epic final between Roger Novacronos and Rafa Nal.
Papa Rodríguez "Tennis is the only sport where you play alone against the world, son. So be a tiger."
Mama Rodríguez "Oh god, these matches are going to kill me!"
Little Yadi touches the bandana Papa placed on his head. Huge smile. His eyes reflect the screen's light.
"That was the day I knew tennis was my thing. Not because it was easy. Because it was impossible."
Page 7 — The Rodríguez Family Dinner
Small table. Warm light. Salsa music from a neighbor's house.
Papa Rodríguez "How was your backhand today? Better than yesterday?"
Yadi "I think so. Don Emilio almost fell off his bench."
Papa Rodríguez "Thinking isn't enough. Tomorrow I want you to know it."
Mama Rodríguez "If you keep this up you're going to have lizard hands. Eat more, you're too skinny."
Page 8 — Yadi's Room at Night
Yadi's small room — one bed, an old poster of Novacronos on the wall, another of Rafa Nal, and a magazine clipping of Luna Borín with her gold medal. Tropical breeze through the open window. He places the bandana on the nightstand and touches it with his fingers.
Mama Rodríguez "Do you think he'll make it far?"
Mama whispers from the doorway, watching Yadi without him noticing.
Papa Rodríguez "That kid has something. He just needs someone to see it."
Yadi asleep. The bandana on the nightstand. The Novacronos poster and Luna Borín photo on the wall lit by moonlight. Sound of coquíes outside. The feeling of big dreams in a small room on a small island.
Page 9 — The Public Courts — Yadi Meets Yen
Next day. The public courts at the Municipal Sports Center. 4 cement courts, better maintained than the island's. Yadi arrives with his plastic bag and racket — eyes wide. These courts are better than anything he's ever seen.
On one of the courts, a young man — Yen, 23 — practices alone. Tall, lean, ponytail, towel on shoulder. Hits the ball with clean technique but no emotion. Mechanical.
Yadi: "He's good... really good."
Yen notices Yadi watching through the fence. Pauses. Looks him up and down — notes the old racket, the worn clothes. No judgment, just observation.
Yen "You playing or just watching?"
Yadi "Me?"
Yen "I don't see anyone else here."
Yen tosses a ball to Yadi over the net. Yadi catches it. Looks at Yen. Looks at the court. His expression changes — from surprise to subtle determination.
Yadi "Let's go."
Pages 11-12 — The Match
Yen serves. Clean, precise. Yadi barely reacts — the ball passes. 15-0.
Second serve. Yadi returns this time, but short. Yen puts away an easy winner. Quick montage: Yen dominates. His technique is superior in every way — serve, forehand, backhand, positioning. Yadi runs everything but can't compete. 3-0.
Yen: "He's fast... and he doesn't give up. But he's very raw."
Yadi at the back of the court, sweating, frustrated but not beaten. Grips the racket harder. Rally — 4 shots, 5, 6. Yadi starts reading the pattern. Yen always goes cross-court after a center shot.
Yen hits to the center. Yadi is already moving before Yen goes cross-court.
Page 12 — The Supernatural Shot
Yadi hits a passing shot down the line. The angle is impossible — the ball passes brushing the net, hits the line, and rockets away. Yen doesn't reach it.
WHACK!!
The ball lands inside the line. A point for Yadi. But it's not a normal point. The speed, the angle, the anticipation — everything was supernatural.
Yadi's eyes — for an instant, a FLASH. A glow that wasn't there before. Like a diamond reflecting light. It lasted half a second. Yadi didn't even notice.
Yen: "What... was... that?"
Pages 13-14 — After the Match
The match continues. Yen wins easily — 6-1 — but he's distracted. Keeps looking at Yadi between points. Yadi hits another good shot — not as spectacular as the previous one, but there are flashes of something.
End of match. They shake hands at the net.
Yadi "Thanks for the match. You're really good."
Yen "Yeah... you've got something too."
Yadi "Something?"
Yen doesn't respond. Just looks at him.
Page 15 — Closing
Yadi picks up balls on the other side of the court, humming, not knowing he just changed his life. Relaxed.
Yen in the foreground, towel in hand, watching Yadi from afar. Serious expression. Analytical. Then a half-smile forming.
Yen: "What was that...? That kid... doesn't even know what he has."

"The Truth That Hurts"
Chapter 2 — Act 1: Roots
"The Truth That Hurts"
Yen seeks out Yadi and proposes training together. Through weeks of brutal training and unfiltered honesty, Yen transforms Yadi's mentality. By the end, Yadi surpasses Yen in level and receives the news that will change everything: there's a National Junior Tournament.
Page 1 — Yen Was Waiting
Next day. Public courts at the Municipal Sports Center. Morning. Yadi arrives as always — plastic bag, old racket. He stops. On the court where they played yesterday, Yen is already there. Sitting on the bench, towel on shoulder, waiting. As if he knew Yadi would come.
Yadi "Were you waiting for me?"
Yen "Don't flatter yourself. I come every day."
Pause.
Yen "But yeah. I was waiting for you. Let's play."
Page 2 — The Demolition
They play. Yen wins the first point easily. Second point — tests Yadi with a drop shot. Yadi runs but doesn't make it. Third point — moves him side to side. Yadi runs everything but his shots are desperate.
After 20 minutes, the score is demolishing. Yadi leaning on his knees, sweating, exhausted. Yen isn't even sweating. Approaches the net:
Yen "Do you want me to be honest with you?"
Yadi "Yes."
Pages 3-4 — The First Truth
Yen and Yadi sitting on the bench at the edge of the court. Water bottles. Silence for a moment.
Yen "You have something I never had. Something that can't be trained. I saw it yesterday — that shot, that angle... that can't be taught."
Yen "But right now you're a bomb without a detonator. All that talent... and your head sabotages you every 5 minutes. You get frustrated, you lose focus, you worry about things you can't control. If you don't fix that, you're going to be another kid with potential who never amounted to anything."
Yadi clenches his fists. Eyes wet — not from sadness, from frustration. It hurts because he knows it's true.
Yadi "And what do you know? You don't know me."
Yen "I know you carry a racket held together with tape and sneakers with the sole falling off. I know you come from an island where nobody plays tennis. And I know that yesterday you hit a shot that I, with 7 years of formal training, can't hit."
Silence. Yadi looks at him.
Yen "Train with me. I'm not going to teach you how to hit the ball — you already know that. I'm going to teach you how to use your head."
Page 5 — Why Would You Help Me?
Yadi "Why would you help me?"
Yen stands up, puts the towel on his shoulder. Looks at the court.
Yen "Because I have the mind but not your talent. You have the talent but not the mind. Between the two of us, we could make one complete player."
E6 — The Handshake That Changed Everything
Yen extends his hand to Yadi. Yadi looks at it. Takes it. Firm handshake.
"I didn't know that handshake was going to change my life."
Pages 6-7 — Training Montage — Weeks Compressed
Yen blindfolds Yadi. Throws balls at him. Yadi must listen to the ball and react.
Yen "Tennis isn't just what you see. It's what you feel."
Yadi meditating on the court. Eyes closed. Yen timing him. Yadi fidgets.
Yen "3 minutes without thinking about anything. Can you?"
Yadi "...No."
Yen "Again."
Yen blasts music from an old speaker while they play. Reggaetón, rock, noise. Yadi can't concentrate, misses everything.
Yen "If you can't play with noise, you can't play in a stadium."
Yadi running at dawn. Alone. The island streets still dark, palm trees like shadows against the sky. Papa Rodríguez watches him leave from the window. Nods silently.
Yen and Yadi playing in the rain. Yadi slips. Gets up. Keeps going.
Yen "You're worrying again. See that ball? That ball doesn't know you're poor. Doesn't know you're scared. It only knows where you send it."
Yadi hitting against the wall with Luna Borín's mural at night, on the island court. Alone. Determined. The mark on the wall is getting deeper and deeper.
Close-up of Yadi's eyes during a training rally — more focused, calmer. The flash in his eyes appears again, this time a bit more visible.
Mama Rodríguez "Son, is that Yen training you or torturing you?"
Papa Rodríguez "He's getting better. You can tell."
Page 8 — Something is Different
Weeks later. Public court. Yadi and Yen playing. But something is different — Yadi moves with more fluidity, more looseness. He smiles on the court. For the first time, he's having FUN during a match with Yen. Not frustrated, not tense.
The flash in his eyes appears more often — brief flashes during rallies. Yen notices but says nothing.
Yen: "He's changing... faster than I expected."
Pages 9-10 — Yen's Secret
After a training session. Sitting in the Sports Center stands, sharing water.
Yadi "Hey, Yen... why did you stop competing?"
Yen looks at his knee. The scar. Long silence.
Yen "My knee decided for me. But my head had already given up before that."
Yen "That's why I tell you to fix the head first. Because the body can fail you, but if your mind is strong... you always find a way."
Yadi "Do you regret it?"
Yen "Every day."
Pause.
Yen "But if I can make sure you don't have to regret anything... maybe that's worth something."
Silence. Yadi looks at the sky. Yen looks at the ground. Two people who need each other without saying it. Panoramic of both sitting in the empty stands. Small in the frame. But together.
Pages 11-13 — Yadi Beats Yen for the First Time
Weeks later. Court. Practice match. Yadi vs Yen. First set: Yen wins but it cost him. 6-4. Second set: long rally. 15 shots, 20. Both running. But Yadi reads the game, anticipates, and hits a cross-court winner. Second set: Yadi 6-3.
Third set. Crucial point. Yadi serves. Clean serve, with direction. Ace. His eyes flash for an instant. Final rally — 30 shots. Both at their limit. But Yadi is mentally fresher — Yen gets frustrated first.
Yadi hits a perfect drop shot. Yen runs, doesn't make it. The ball bounces twice. Match point. Yadi wins.
Yadi at the net. Doesn't celebrate excessively. Doesn't shout. Looks at Yen with deep respect. Extends his hand.
Yen "About time, idiot."
Both smile. A moment of real camaraderie. Not student-teacher — it's something more. Yen ruffles Yadi's hair. Yadi laughs.
"I surpassed him on the court. But I'll never stop looking up to him."
Pages 14-15 — The Tournament Flyer
Yen sits on the bench. Takes something from his bag — a folded flyer. Opens it and shows it to Yadi. Close-up: "NATIONAL JUNIOR TOURNAMENT — OPEN REGISTRATION". Perfect courts in the image. Sponsor logos. Everything looks big, distant, impossible.
Yen "It's time for the world to see what I saw."
Yadi takes the flyer. His hands tremble. His eyes widen. It's the first time he's seen something like this up close — a real opportunity. Close-up of his eyes reflecting the images of professional courts, trophies, players.
Yadi "Do you think I'm ready?"
Yen, standing, towel on shoulder, looking at the horizon. Defiant smile:
Yen "No. But you're going anyway."

"The World Out There"
Yadi leaves his island for the first time to compete in the National Junior Tournament. Brutal culture shock: perfect courts, rich players, elite teams. We meet the three main rivals — Daimon Richwell, Kai Morimoto, and Luca Santori. Yadi is verbally humiliated by Daimon but channels his rage into determination.
Pages 1-2 — The Farewell
Exterior of the Rodríguez house. Dawn. Small suitcase at the door. Yadi with his usual bag and an old backpack.
Mama Rodríguez hands him something wrapped in recycled gift paper. Teary eyes but smiling:
Mama Rodríguez "Here. It's not new, but it has all my love."
Yadi opens the package — a used racket but in much better condition than his. New grip, freshly strung. Yadi touches it like it's treasure.
Mama Rodríguez "If you break it, I'll break you."
Yadi hugs her tight. She squeezes harder.
Papa Rodríguez "Go and be a tiger. Don't be afraid of anyone."
Handshake between father and son — strong, masculine, full of everything they don't say with words. Mama is openly crying now. Papa too, but hides it looking away and coughing.
Mama Rodríguez "Call me when you get there! And eat well! And don't stay up late!"
Yadi walking down the colorful island street toward where Yen waits with an old car. The Rodríguez house behind, painted in faded sky blue, with his parents at the door.
Don Emilio "Go, kid. This court is too small for you now."
E10 — Mama's Racket — The Gift
A used racket but in much better condition — new grip, freshly strung. All of mama's love wrapped in recycled gift paper.
Page 3 — The Journey
Yen's car on an open road. Enormous sky. Yadi looks out the window. The new racket rests on his lap — he keeps touching it. Yen drives in silence.
Yadi "Yen... what's it like?"
Yen "What's what like?"
Yadi "There. The tournament. The other players."
Yen "Different. They have everything you don't. Except what matters."
Pages 4-5 — Arrival at Youth Arena
The National Junior Club — "Youth Arena." Yadi and Yen standing in front of the entrance. Modern building, clean, bright. Perfect courts visible through glass. Sponsor logos everywhere. Yadi looks tiny in front of all this.
Interior. Lobby. Players in brand-name clothes, professional bags, latest-generation rackets — 3 or 4 each. Coaches with tablets, nutritionists with clipboards. A completely different world.
Yadi in the middle of it all, with his old backpack, mama's new racket, and worn-out athletic clothes. The stares. Subtle but real. Some see him and look away. Others murmur.
Yen "They've already started judging you. Good. Let them. You didn't come here to make friends."
Yadi: "This is real."
Page 6 — The Rivals
DAIMON RICHWELL — #1 National Junior Ranking — "The Golden Boy." Arrives in a luxury car. Gets out like he's famous. Blonde, perfect skin, brand-name clothes, 4 rackets in a professional bag. Entourage: personal coach, physiotherapist, an assistant carrying his things. Arrogant smile.
KAI MORIMOTO — #3 National Junior Ranking — "The Silent Wall." Sitting alone in a corner of the lobby. Short, stocky, completely impassive expression. Reading something on his phone. Doesn't talk to anyone. Doesn't need anyone.
LUCA SANTORI — #5 National Junior Ranking — "The Viper." Leaning against a pillar, watching everyone who enters with a permanent smile that doesn't reach his eyes. Thin, narrow eyes, calculating.
And in the background — Yadi. No introduction text. Invisible.
Pages 8-9 — Daimon's Provocation
Yadi in the locker rooms, finding his locker. Opens his old backpack — clothes carefully folded by mama, a small towel, papa's bandana, and a tupperware with homemade food. Behind him, a group approaches. Laughter.
Daimon Richwell. Standing in front of him with two companions. Looks Yadi up and down. Stops at the racket. Stops at the sneakers. Stops at the clothes.
Daimon "You? With that racket?"
Laughter from the group. Another player:
Crowd "This isn't charity, right?"
Daimon "Listen, this is professional tennis. I don't know how you got in, but... good luck."
Yadi doesn't respond. Stands still. Fists clenched at his sides. Jaw tight. Contained rage — but he doesn't explode.
Yen appears behind Yadi. Calm voice, hand on his shoulder:
Yen "Save that rage. You'll need it on the court."
Daimon looks at Yen, dismisses him with a gesture, and leaves with his group. Yadi exhales.
Yen "You okay?"
Yadi "I'm furious."
Yen "Good. Now turn that into points."
Page 7 — The Tupperware Note
Note inside the tupperware, mama's handwriting:
"Eat everything. I love you. Win or lose, you're my champion." — Mama
Below, in papa's handwriting, added with a different pen:

Main hall. Big screen with the tournament bracket. All players gathered, watching. Murmurs, analysis, nerves. Yadi's name in the bottom half. Clear path: if he advances, he faces a lesser rival in the first round, then potentially Kai Morimoto, then Luca Santori, and Daimon would wait in the zone final.
Yadi: "If I want to reach him... I have to win everything."
Yen "Did you see the bracket?"
Yadi "Yeah. Daimon's at the top."
Yen "Scared?"
Yadi "Hungry."
Yen "That's the right answer."
Pages 12-13 — Yadi Alone on the Perfect Court at Night
The empty courts at night. Artificial lights on at a single court. The rest in darkness. Yadi alone on the lit court. Mama's new racket in hand. Papa's bandana on his forehead. Hitting balls. His shots sound different here — cleaner on this surface. It feels good. It feels right.
He stops. Looks at the empty court in front of him. The perfect lines. The taut net. The lights. Imagines the stands full. For an instant, Yadi sees himself on a center court, with thousands of people. In front of him, enormous shadows — the silhouettes of the great players. The image fades. Back to reality. Alone. Empty court.
Takes out his phone. Calls home. Mama answers before the second ring:
Mama Rodríguez "Son?! How are you?! Did you eat?! What are the courts like?!"
Yadi "I'm fine, ma. The courts are incredible."
Papa takes the phone:
Papa Rodríguez "Did you see the other players?"

"I didn't come here to participate. I came here to make them know who I am."

"First Blood"
Yadi's first official tournament match. Nerves, initial disaster, and the first live transformation. Yadi learns to let go of fear and play free, coming back from a match that seemed lost. The circuit starts to notice something different about this unknown kid.
Page 1 — Pre-Match Nerves — Bandana Ritual
Morning of the first tournament day. Yadi in the locker room, alone. Sitting on the bench facing his open locker. Looks at the racket. Looks at his hands. Hands trembling slightly. Closes his eyes.
Yen's voice: "Don't think about winning. Think about each ball. One at a time."
Opens his eyes. Takes out papa's bandana. Puts it on in front of the mirror. Looks at himself. Breathes.
Yadi: "Be a tiger."
Object — Papa's Bandana
Papa's old bandana — faded, frayed, with faint tiger stripes. A ritual now.
Page 2 — Entering the Court
Yadi walks out into the hallway leading to the courts. Light at the end. Sound of balls and crowd. From the stands, Yen watches him walk toward the court. Arms crossed. Neutral face.
Yadi enters the court. Secondary court, not center, but the biggest thing he's seen up close. Scattered crowd — about 200 people. For Yadi, it might as well be 20,000.
His rival is already there: player #15 in the rankings. Perfect uniform, professional bag, coach in his corner. Everything Yadi doesn't have.
Pages 3-4 — First Set: Disaster
Yadi serves. The serve goes long. Fault. Second serve — weak, to the center. The rival hits a return winner. 0-3. The crowd murmurs. Some laugh quietly:
Crowd "Where did this guy come from?"
In the stands, Daimon watches with arms crossed. Dismissive smile:
Daimon "This is the one who was going to face me? What a waste."
0-4. His body is rigid. His mind is everywhere: on Daimon, on the crowd, on what they'll think if he loses. On everything except the ball.
Changeover. Yadi sitting in his chair. Towel on his face. Hands still trembling. Looks toward the stands. Sees Yen. Yen doesn't signal, doesn't shout. Just looks at him. Steady. Calm.
Yadi lowers his gaze. Sees mama's note peeking from his pocket. Thinks of papa:
Papa's voice: "You lost? Good. Now tell me what you'll do differently."
First set: 6-2 for the rival. Yadi stands up from the chair. Looks defeated... but something is changing.
Page 5 — Yadi Smiles for the First Time
Start of the second set. Yadi positions to receive serve. Closes his eyes for a second.
Yen's voice: "When everything goes against you, let go. Stop fighting yourself. Play."
Yadi opens his eyes. Something is different. His posture relaxes. Shoulders drop. Jaw loosens. And then — Yadi smiles. The first smile of the match. Not forced — genuine. As if he suddenly remembered this is a game. That it's supposed to be FUN.
Yadi: "Let go. Just let go."
Pages 6-8 — The Transformation
The rival serves. Yadi returns — but different. The shot is loose, natural, with angle. The rival is surprised. Rally — 6 shots. Yadi moves with fluidity. No longer fighting himself. Reads the game. Anticipates. Winner from Yadi down the line. Clean. Fast. 15-0.
Yadi's eyes have a subtle flash. The Diamond Vision is starting to awaken.
Yen: "There it is."
Point montage: Yadi winning game after game. His shots are cleaner, more aggressive, with angles that shouldn't work but do. The rival starts getting frustrated. Looks at his coach seeking answers. The coach has none.
Yadi 5-1 in the second set. The crowd is silent — but a different silence. No longer mockery. It's attention.
Point to close the second set. 12-shot rally. Yadi dominating. His eyes flash intermittently. Yadi closes the set with a cross-court forehand winner that hits the exact corner.
CRACK!!
Second set: Yadi 6-1. The rival looks at Yadi as if he were another person. Because he is. The Yadi from the first set died at the changeover.
Pages 10-11 — The Comeback Victory
Third set. The rival tries to react but Yadi has found his rhythm. 5-2 Yadi. Match point. Final rally — 8 shots. Yadi finds the angle. Forehand down the line. The ball travels fast, low, grazing the net... and lands in.
Point. Set. Match. Yadi wins: 2-6, 6-1, 6-3.
The rival crosses the net. Shakes his hand:
Crowd "Good match, kid. In the second set... what happened to you?"
Yadi "I don't know. I just stopped thinking."
Yadi from behind, walking toward the court exit. The scattered crowd applauds — with genuine respect. The murmur has changed:
Crowd "Who is that kid?"
Crowd "Where did he come from?"
Crowd "Did you see that second set?"

Daimon in the stands. No longer smiling. Frowns for the first time. His coach whispers something. Daimon doesn't respond.
Kai Morimoto, sitting alone as always. But now looking toward the court where Yadi played. His eyes, for the first time, show a spark of interest.
Luca: "Interesting..."
Yen in the stands. Arms crossed again. Nodding imperceptibly. The slightest smile at the corner of his mouth.

Split image — Yadi on the left looking at the bracket, Kai on the right on the court. An imaginary line connects them. Two worlds about to collide.
Kai: "Yadi Rodríguez. Let's see what you've got."

"The Wall"
Chapter 5 — Act 2: The Fire
"The Wall"
Yadi faces Kai Morimoto, the defensive player who returns absolutely everything. Yadi learns the hardest lesson yet: talent without strategy isn't enough. After losing a first set out of frustration, Yadi evolves his game and discovers new facets — variation, net play, tactical intelligence.
Pages 1-2 — Yen's Warning
Morning of day two. Yadi on the warm-up court, hitting balls with confidence. Feels good after yesterday's victory. Yen approaches. His expression doesn't share Yadi's optimism. Serious:
Yen "We need to talk about your next rival."
Yadi "Kai Morimoto, right? The #3. I already saw him. He doesn't seem that intimidating."
Yen "That kid doesn't have your talent. But he has something worse: infinite patience. He's a wall. Returns everything. Everything. And if you play his game, you lose."
Yadi "So what do I do?"
Yen "I'm not going to tell you what to do. I'll tell you what NOT to do: don't get frustrated. Don't force. Don't play faster than you need to. He wants you to get desperate. That's his weapon."
Yadi nods. But in his eyes you can see he doesn't fully understand. Thinks his talent will be enough.
Pages 3-5 — Kai Returns Everything
Both on the court. Kai on the other side: short, stocky, completely expressionless. Doesn't look at Yadi. Only looks at the ball. Yadi serves. Good serve. Kai returns it — no power, no style, but perfect placement. Deep to the center.
Yadi attacks. Cross-court forehand with angle. A shot that yesterday would be a winner...
Kai is there. Returns it. No apparent effort. The ball comes back like a mirror.
Yadi: "How did he get there?"
Rally. 10 shots. 15. 20. Yadi hits harder each time. Kai returns everything. No emotion. No tiring. Like a robot. Yadi forces a shot — too hard, too much angle. The ball goes out by centimeters. Unforced error. Kai doesn't celebrate. Doesn't react. Just walks to his position. The coldness is terrifying.
Point montage: Yadi attacking with everything — powerful forehands, cross-court backhands, drop shots. Every shot looks like a winner... and Kai returns ALL of them. Over and over and over. The visual effect is suffocating — like a wall that never breaks.
Yadi starting to get frustrated. Shouts after an error. Slaps the racket against his leg. Score: Kai 5-3, first set. Yadi is forcing everything. His shots are more powerful but less intelligent. Exactly what Kai wants.
First set: Kai 7-5. Yadi lost while hitting more winners than Kai, but making triple the errors. Yen was right.
Yadi: "I'm hitting harder than anyone and it doesn't work. What do I do? What do I do?"
Page 7 — Yen Shouts: "Fight Yourself!"
